## The Problem: From Tokens to Meaning

In our last lesson, we learned how to break text into tokens. But there's still a fundamental problem: **how do we represent the meaning of words as numbers?**

Consider these tokens from a product review:
```
["This", "phone", "is", "amazing", "!"]
```

We could assign each word a simple ID number:
- "This" = 1
- "phone" = 2  
- "is" = 3
- "amazing" = 4
- "!" = 5

But this approach has a critical flaw: **it treats all words as equally different**. The computer has no way to know that:
- "phone" and "smartphone" are related
- "amazing" and "fantastic" have similar meanings
- "cat" and "dog" are both animals

**Word embeddings solve this by placing semantically similar words close together in a mathematical vector space.**

## Real-World Example: Amazon Product Recommendations

Let's see how word embeddings power Amazon's product recommendation system.

### The Challenge
When you search for "wireless headphones," Amazon needs to understand that you might also be interested in:
- "bluetooth earbuds" 
- "noise-canceling headsets"
- "wireless audio devices"

Without embeddings, these would be completely different searches with no connection.

### Step 1: Learning from Context (Word2Vec)

Word2Vec learns word meanings by analyzing how words appear together in product descriptions:

<WordEmbeddingExplorer 
  defaultWords={['headphones', 'earbuds', 'bluetooth', 'wireless', 'audio', 'music']}
  showSimilarity={true}
  highlightRelationships={true}
/>

**The Key Insight**: Words that appear in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings.

From millions of product descriptions like:
- "Wireless **headphones** with **bluetooth** technology"
- "**Bluetooth** **earbuds** for wireless **music**"
- "Noise-canceling **headphones** for **audio** enthusiasts"

Word2Vec learns that "headphones," "earbuds," and "bluetooth" are semantically related because they consistently appear together.

### Step 2: Vector Mathematics Captures Meaning

Once trained, these relationships become mathematical operations:

```
vector("headphones") + vector("wireless") ≈ vector("bluetooth_headphones")
vector("earbuds") + vector("noise-canceling") ≈ vector("noise_canceling_earbuds")
```

This allows Amazon to recommend products even when the search terms don't exactly match the product descriptions.

## Word2Vec: Learning from Local Context

Word2Vec revolutionized NLP by learning embeddings from word co-occurrence patterns. There are two main approaches:

### Skip-gram: Predicting Context from Words

Given the sentence: "The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog"

Skip-gram asks: *"If I see the word 'fox', what other words are likely to appear nearby?"*

<Word2VecDemo 
  sentence="The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog"
  targetWord="fox"
  windowSize={2}
  method="skipgram"
/>

**Training Process:**
1. Choose a target word: "fox"
2. Look at surrounding words within a window: ["quick", "brown", "jumps", "over"]  
3. Train to predict these context words
4. Adjust the vector for "fox" to better predict its typical neighbors

### CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words): Predicting Words from Context

CBOW flips this around, asking: *"Given the context words ['quick', 'brown', 'jumps', 'over'], what word appears in the middle?"*

<Word2VecDemo 
  sentence="The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog"
  targetWord="fox"
  windowSize={2}
  method="cbow"
/>

**Why This Works:**
- Words with similar meanings appear in similar contexts
- "King" and "queen" both appear near words like "royal," "crown," "palace"
- "Running" and "jogging" both appear near "fast," "exercise," "fitness"

## GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation

While Word2Vec focuses on local context windows, GloVe takes a different approach by analyzing **global statistical information** across the entire corpus.

### The Global Statistics Approach

GloVe builds a **word co-occurrence matrix** that captures how often words appear together across all documents:

<GloVeVisualization 
  corpus={[
    "I love ice cream on hot days",
    "Ice cream is cold and sweet", 
    "Hot weather makes me crave cold drinks",
    "Sweet treats are perfect for hot summer days"
  ]}
  showCooccurrenceMatrix={true}
  targetWords={['ice', 'cream', 'hot', 'cold']}
/>

**Key Insight**: The ratio of co-occurrence probabilities reveals semantic relationships.

For example:
- P(solid|ice) / P(solid|steam) is large (ice is often solid, steam is not)
- P(gas|steam) / P(gas|ice) is large (steam is often gas, ice is not)  
- P(water|ice) / P(water|steam) ≈ 1 (both relate to water equally)

### Combining Local and Global Information

GloVe optimizes embeddings to satisfy:
```
word_i · word_j = log(P(i appears with j))
```

This means the dot product between word vectors equals the logarithm of their co-occurrence probability.

## The Magic of Vector Arithmetic

Once trained, word embeddings reveal surprising semantic relationships through simple vector mathematics:

<VectorArithmeticDemo 
  examples={[
    {operation: "king - man + woman", result: "queen", explanation: "Gender relationship transfer"},
    {operation: "paris - france + italy", result: "rome", explanation: "Capital city relationships"},
    {operation: "good - bad + terrible", result: "excellent", explanation: "Sentiment intensity mapping"},
    {operation: "walking - walk + run", result: "running", explanation: "Grammatical form consistency"}
  ]}
/>

**Why This Works:**
- Vector differences capture **relationships** (king - man = "royalty without male gender")
- Vector addition applies those relationships (+ woman = "royalty with female gender")
- The result points to the word that best embodies that combined meaning

## Comparing Word2Vec vs GloVe

| Aspect | Word2Vec | GloVe |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **Training Data** | Local context windows | Global co-occurrence statistics |
| **Computation** | Neural network (slower) | Matrix factorization (faster) |
| **Memory Usage** | Lower during training | Higher (stores full matrix) |
| **Performance** | Better on semantic tasks | Better on syntactic tasks |
| **Rare Words** | Struggles with infrequent words | Better handling via global stats |

<ModelComparisonDemo 
  models={['word2vec', 'glove']}
  tasks={['semantic_similarity', 'analogy_completion', 'syntactic_relationships']}
  showPerformance={true}
/>

**When to Use Each:**
- **Word2Vec**: When you have limited computational resources and care most about semantic relationships
- **GloVe**: When you want faster training and better syntactic understanding

## Impact on Modern AI Systems

Word embeddings revolutionized NLP and remain fundamental to modern AI:

### Direct Applications
- **Search engines**: Understanding query intent and document relevance
- **Recommendation systems**: Finding similar products or content
- **Machine translation**: Aligning words across languages
- **Sentiment analysis**: Capturing emotional nuances in text

### Foundation for Modern Models
- **BERT**: Uses contextualized embeddings (words have different vectors in different contexts)
- **GPT**: Builds on embedding principles for text generation
- **ChatGPT**: Leverages semantic understanding for conversation

**Evolution Path:**
Static Embeddings (Word2Vec/GloVe) → Contextualized Embeddings (BERT) → Large Language Models (GPT) → Modern AI Assistants
